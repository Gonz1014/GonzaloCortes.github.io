CarJack: CAD & Assembly

CAD model, 3D print, laser, CNC, Milling

  • I built a gear-synchronized scissor lift to demonstrate smooth, symmetric motion from one input. I drove it like an engineering build: fix the critical interfaces, tolerance from those, and use serviceable hardware so the mechanism feels precise and intentional.
  • Workflow: modeled everything in SolidWorks (parametric links, sector gears, lead-screw travel), generated CAM, rough-cut stock on the bandsaw, then CNC-milled and faced the acrylic bearing blocks. I laser-cut and 3D-printed link and gear prototypes, profiled finals, reamed all bores for shoulder screws and dowel pins, tapped the nut and hubs, deburred, dry-fit, and tuned mesh until there was zero bind.
  • The true challenge was acrylic. It chips and melts fast, so I ran single-flute tools, light step-downs, and constant coolant. One lapse ruins the part—any late-pass miscalculation meant remaking a block from scratch.

Blackjack & Video Poker (Java/JavaFX)

Engines, GUI

  • The original was a super-simple Intro to Java final project console poker variant (no true Jacks-or-Better rules, clunky text I/O), so gameplay felt limited and the code wasn’t structured for reuse.
  • I wanted to push project into something I’d actually play and showcase, apply real OOP and ship a desktop UI.
  • I refactored into reusable engines (Card, Deck, HandEvaluator, PayTable), upgraded to true Jacks-or-Better with correct payouts, then added Blackjack via a compact turn/state machine and dealer logic.
  • I disliked the terminal experience, so I kept the core UI-agnostic and taught myself JavaFX to build a clean GUI (tokens/bets, hit/stand/double, draw/hold, action log); that process cemented MVC separation, deterministic state flow, and robust edge-case handling (ace 1/11, naturals, reshuffle), turning it into a polished, playable app.

Custom Mountain Bike: Frame-Up Build

Component selection, compatibility, assembly

  • There was a period where Bike prices jumped and the price of individual components stayed the same. I saw a window to build a better trail bike at a cheaper cost than from the source. Doing it myself let me choose what components truly mattered to me and which could be cost effective. I ended up with a quieter, better fitting, and more to my liking ride without paying for compromises.
  • I began with the four pillars and let everything else follow. Good frame, good fork, good brakes, good shifter/derailleur. I locked the key interfaces first so the system would work as one piece. From there I filled in the supporting choices that best matched those standards and my trails, like tires, wheels, cassette, crank length, cockpit, and dropper travel.
  • The hardest parts taught me patience. Internal routing was far more involved than expected and hydraulic hoses required trimming and a full bleed. I uncovered a bottom bracket surprise when the frame was threaded but the crankset needed a different unit, so I sourced the correct threaded bottom bracket that matched the spindle and shell. I also had to cut the fork steerer to the measured stack, set the star nut, and preload the headset correctly.
  • I learned to define standards first and choose everything around those decisions. Measure twice and cut once, especially for steerers and hoses. Plan for a bleed whenever lines are trimmed. Quiet and reliable bikes come from details like clean routing, correct torque, and careful setup, not only from price tags.
