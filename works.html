Chromie: SMS Scheduling Assistant

Twilio with Flask with Calendar Manager

  • Hospitals were drowning in coverage gaps and group texts. At my time in Chromie, I built an SMS-driven swap system that lets staff fill openings in minutes with no app to download, while managers keep full oversight and every action is time-stamped and auditable.
  • The website had a very simple structure. Upload a monthly CSV, see shifts on a calendar, tap a date to reach the right people, and staff reply in natural language to claim or offer. I fine-tuned a compact AI model to understand messy replies (names, dates, claim vs offer vs cancel) and fall back to guided prompts when uncertain. Managers can approve by SMS or on the website. Deployed on Render with a Flask API, PostgreSQL as the source of truth, and Twilio for messaging. Email invites onboard managers securely. The program is multi-tenant, with strict row-level scoping so multiple organizations run side-by-side without any data leakage.
  • The hard part came from keeping web, SMS, and database perfectly in sync and eliminating double claims. I designed a clear state machine for offers and approvals, used idempotent tokens and transactional writes, verified webhooks, and timezone-aware timestamps. Resulted in fast swaps, clean audit history, and reliable operations across many teams.

CarJack: CAD & Assembly

CAD model, 3D print, laser, CNC, Milling

  • I built a gear-synchronized scissor lift to demonstrate smooth, symmetric motion from one input. I drove it like an engineering build: fix the critical interfaces, tolerance from those, and use serviceable hardware so the mechanism feels precise and intentional.
  • Modeled everything in SolidWorks (parametric links, sector gears, lead-screw travel), generated CAM, rough-cut stock on the bandsaw, then CNC-milled and faced the acrylic bearing blocks. I laser-cut and 3D-printed link and gear prototypes, profiled finals, reamed all bores for shoulder screws and dowel pins, tapped the nut and hubs, deburred, dry-fit, and tuned mesh until there was zero bind.
  • The true challenge was acrylic. It chips and melts fast, so I ran single-flute tools, light step-downs, and constant coolant. One lapse ruins the part—any late-pass miscalculation meant remaking a block from scratch.

Blackjack & Video Poker (Java/JavaFX)

Engines, GUI

  • The original was a super-simple Intro to Java final project console poker variant (no true Jacks-or-Better rules, clunky text I/O), so gameplay felt limited and the code wasn’t structured for reuse.
  • I wanted to push project into something I’d actually play and showcase, apply real OOP and ship a desktop UI.
  • I refactored into reusable engines (Card, Deck, HandEvaluator, PayTable), upgraded to true Jacks-or-Better with correct payouts, then added Blackjack via a compact turn/state machine and dealer logic.
  • I disliked the terminal experience, so I kept the core UI-agnostic and taught myself JavaFX to build a clean GUI (tokens/bets, hit/stand/double, draw/hold, action log); that process cemented MVC separation, deterministic state flow, and robust edge-case handling (ace 1/11, naturals, reshuffle), turning it into a polished, playable app.

Custom Mountain Bike: Frame-Up Build

Component selection, compatibility, assembly

  • Bike prices spiked while component prices held steady, so I took the chance to build a better trail bike for less. Choosing what mattered most let me get a quieter, better-fitting ride without paying for compromises.
  • I started with four pillars: good frame, good fork, good brakes, good shifter and derailleur. Then locked the interfaces so everything worked as one system. From there, I chose tires, wheels, cassette, crank length, cockpit, and dropper to suit those choices and my trails.
  • The hardest parts taught me patience. Internal routing was far more involved than expected and hydraulic hoses required trimming and a full bleed. I uncovered a bottom bracket surprise when the frame was threaded but the crankset needed a different unit, so I sourced the correct threaded bottom bracket that matched the spindle and shell. I also had to cut the fork steerer to the measured stack, set the star nut, and preload the headset correctly.
  • I learned to define standards first and build around them; measure twice and cut once on steerers and hoses; plan to bleed after any hose trim. Quiet, reliable bikes come from clean routing, correct torque, and careful setup, not just price.
